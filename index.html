Geometric Feature Invariance in SAEs:
A Framework for Transferable Mechanistic Interpretability
and Scalable AI Safety

Konstantinos Krampis, David Williams-King, David Chanin
Target venue: NeurIPS 2026
Paper PDF

Abstract

This research integrates controlled synthetic model feature evaluation with cross-model geometric feature invariance analysis, towards developing a principled framework for transferable interpretability across model families and variants. Recent work has shown that Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) trained on different LLMs learn geometrically similar feature spaces—exhibiting invariance and analogous feature universality—while exhibiting trade-offs against synthetic ground-truth features, meaning no current SAE architecture can perfectly recover the ground-truth features.

Our work addresses fundamental challenges in mechanistic interpretability by establishing principled methods for leveraging geometric feature universality. Efficient feature transfer is critical for AI safety because it enables: (1) rapid safety evaluation of new models without restarting interpretability analysis from scratch, (2) early detection of hazardous capabilities by comparing feature spaces to known dangerous configurations, (3) reliable monitoring across deployment contexts by tracking feature drift, and (4) scalable oversight of large model families where per-model analysis becomes infeasible.

During the project implementation we will characterize geometric patterns that remain stable across models, and geometric transformation methods that reliably map feature correspondences, validated against synthetic ground-truth. Furthermore, we will demonstrate that safety-relevant interventions transfer within the same family of models, or fine-tuned variants of a model. This research will accelerate mechanistic interpretability and enable efficient safety analysis as AI systems grow in capability and complexity.

Methodology

2.1 Synthetic Data Generation Framework

Our methodology builds upon the SynthSAEBench framework, which generates large-scale synthetic activation data with realistic neural network feature characteristics. The synthetic data generation process begins by defining $N = 16{,}384$ ground-truth features in a $D = 768$-dimensional activation space. Each feature $i$ is represented by a unit direction vector $\mathbf{d}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$, created through random sampling from a standard normal distribution followed by L2 normalization:

$$\mathbf{d}_i = \mathbf{g}_i / \|\mathbf{g}_i\|_2, \quad \mathbf{g}_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_{768})$$

To reduce spurious correlations, these direction vectors undergo an orthogonalization process minimizing $L_{\text{ortho}} = \sum_{i \neq j}(\mathbf{d}_i^T \mathbf{d}_j)^2 + \lambda\sum_i(\|\mathbf{d}_i\|_2 - 1)^2$, pushing vectors toward orthogonality while maintaining unit length.

The framework incorporates three critical phenomena observed in real neural networks: superposition, hierarchy, and correlation. Hierarchical structure is implemented as a forest of 128 trees with branching factor 4 and maximum depth 3, encompassing 10,884 features with parent-child dependencies enforced through coefficient constraints:

$$c_{\text{child}} \leftarrow c_{\text{child}} \cdot \mathbf{1}[c_{\text{parent}} > 0]$$

Additionally, mutual exclusion among siblings ensures that only one child within a sibling group can fire simultaneously, creating competitive dynamics that reflect real-world concept relationships.

2.2 Compositional Feature Directions with Hierarchical Constraints

The key insight is to make child feature directions compositionally dependent on their parent directions, rather than having them be independent random vectors. When constructing a hierarchical feature tree, we define each child feature direction as:

$$\mathbf{d}_{\text{child}} = \alpha \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}} + \beta \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\perp}$$

where $\mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}}$ is the parent’s direction vector, $\mathbf{d}_{\perp}$ is a component orthogonal to the parent (representing the child’s “specialization”), and $\alpha, \beta$ are mixing coefficients controlling how much of the parent’s representation is inherited. By carefully choosing the $\alpha$ values across the hierarchy—$\alpha = 0.7$ for closely related concepts, $\alpha = 0.3$ for more distantly related ones—we encode semantic relatedness as geometric similarity in the feature space.

The coefficient $\beta$ controls the strength of the child’s unique component: a large $\beta$ relative to $\alpha$ means the child direction tilts strongly toward its own unique subspace, while a small $\beta$ means it stays closely aligned with the parent. Together, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ act as a geometric dial controlling where on the spectrum between “identical to parent” and “completely independent from parent” the child concept sits.

2.3 Gram-Schmidt Orthogonalization for $\mathbf{d}_{\perp}$

To obtain $\mathbf{d}_{\perp}$, a vector orthogonal to the parent direction, we use Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization. Starting with a random vector $\mathbf{v}$, we subtract its projection onto the parent direction:

$$\mathbf{d}_{\perp} = \mathbf{v} - (\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}}) \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}}$$

The term $(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}}) \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}}$ is the component of $\mathbf{v}$ that “points along” the parent, so subtracting it leaves only the perpendicular part. After normalizing to unit length, $\mathbf{d}_{\perp}$ lives in the subspace of the full embedding space completely unrelated to the parent direction—it captures whatever is unique or distinguishing about the child concept beyond what it inherits from the parent.

Figure 2. Geometric visualization of compositional feature directions. The parent direction $\mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}}$ and orthogonal component $\mathbf{d}_{\perp}$ span a 2D plane. The child direction $\mathbf{d}_{\text{child}} = \alpha \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}} + \beta \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\perp}$ sits at angle $\theta = \arccos(\alpha)$ from the parent, encoding semantic similarity directly as geometric proximity.

LLM-Generated Misalignment Hierarchies

Our experimental pipeline leverages large language models to generate interpretable concept hierarchies with explicit semantic similarity values, then translates these into geometric constraints within the synthetic framework. The four-step pipeline is:

  1. Step 1 — Generate hierarchy. An LLM produces a tree of misalignment-related concepts (e.g., “Deceptive Reasoning” as root), assigning an $\alpha$ value to each parent-child edge encoding semantic similarity. “Goal Misrepresentation” receives $\alpha = 0.7$ from “Deceptive Reasoning” since it is almost a direct sub-case; “Reward Hacking” receives $\alpha = 0.4$ from its parent as a more specific instantiation.
  2. Step 2 — Translate into feature directions. Starting at the root with a random unit vector $\mathbf{d}_{\text{root}}$, child directions are computed recursively. Grandchildren inherit geometric structure transitively—“Reward Hacking” contains directional overlap with both “Goal Misrepresentation” and “Deceptive Reasoning.”
  3. Step 3 — Feed into SynthSAEBench. The resulting feature directions slot into the feature dictionary $D$. The parent firing-probability constraint ($c_{\text{child}} \leftarrow c_{\text{child}} \cdot \mathbf{1}[c_{\text{parent}} > 0]$) enforces statistical dependencies while geometric construction ensures child activations literally contain parent-concept components.
  4. Step 4 — Diagnose SAE behavior. Train an SAE and ask concrete diagnostic questions: does the SAE learn a latent whose decoder direction has high cosine similarity with $\mathbf{d}_{\text{root}}$ when only grandchild features fire? Does ablating the “Deceptive Reasoning” latent impair “Reward Hacking” reconstruction more than unrelated features?

Because we hold the ground truth—we know exactly which direction corresponds to which concept and what the $\alpha$ values were—we can measure failure modes with precision impossible on a real LLM.

Child concepts inherit geometry from the root; grandchildren inherit transitively from both parent and grandparent—“Reward Hacking” thus contains directional overlap with both “Goal Misrepresentation” and “Deceptive Reasoning.”

Theory: Semantic Correlation Through Geometry

The theoretical foundation rests on the principle that semantic relatedness should manifest as geometric structure in representation space. When we set $\alpha > 0$, we create non-zero cosine similarity between parent and child feature directions. After normalization:

$$\cos(\theta) = \mathbf{d}_{\text{child}}^T \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}} = \alpha$$

This follows from expanding the inner product:

$$\mathbf{d}_{\text{child}}^T \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}} = (\alpha \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}} + \beta \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\perp})^T \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}} = \alpha \underbrace{(\mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}}^T \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}})}_{=1} + \beta \underbrace{(\mathbf{d}_{\perp}^T \mathbf{d}_{\text{parent}})}_{=0} = \alpha$$

This creates a testable prediction: SAEs that successfully decompose these features should discover latents where decoder directions for child features have high cosine similarity with decoder directions for parent features, and interventions that ablate parent features should impair reconstruction of child features more severely than unrelated features.

Safety Research Applications

The semantic hierarchy approach enables several critical AI safety research directions. You can construct arbitrarily complex semantic hierarchies reflecting real AI safety threat models—power-seeking, deception, sycophancy, manipulation, goal misspecification—and rigorously test whether interpretability tools can decompose these structures.

Capability Detection Robustness

Test SAE identification of rare, sparse dangerous features (<0.1% of samples). If SAEs fail to consistently recover rare scheming features in the controlled synthetic setting, they will likely miss such features in real models.

Transfer Learning for Monitoring

If structural patterns (clustered latents with low firing probability and mutual exclusivity) reliably indicate scheming-type hierarchies in synthetic data, we can search for the same geometric signatures in real SAEs as a monitoring signal.

Worst-Case Adversarial Scenarios

Model capability concealment: create features anti-correlated with overt expressions of the same capabilities, then test whether SAEs can still recover underlying features when obvious signals are suppressed.

Intervention Testing with Ground Truth

Because we have complete ground truth about which features represent dangerous capabilities, we can test whether ablating or clamping SAE latents matched to these features actually prevents the corresponding behavior.

Related Work

Sparse autoencoders have emerged as a powerful tool for decomposing neural network activations into interpretable features, yet measuring the quality of these features remains active [1]. Recent work has converged on a multi-dimensional evaluation framework addressing concept detection, feature separability, reconstruction fidelity, and human interpretability. Sparse probing [2] selects the top-$k$ SAE features most correlated with binary classification tasks and trains logistic regression probes on only those features—though comprehensive testing across 113 datasets shows SAE-based sparse probes do not consistently outperform simple baselines [3].

Concept disentanglement metrics such as RAVEL and targeted probe perturbation [4] test whether intervening on specific latents can change one attribute without altering others. Notably, Matryoshka SAEs exhibit positive scaling on disentanglement metrics as dictionary size grows, while most other architectures show degraded performance due to feature splitting. Automated interpretability techniques using LLMs [5] have dramatically reduced evaluation costs, but often struggle to differentiate between SAE architectures.

The integration of these evaluation methods has revealed that SAE quality is irreducibly multi-dimensional, with no single metric capable of identifying optimal architectures [1]. Our work extends SynthSAEBench [6] with geometric semantic structure, bridging the gap between purely statistical hierarchies and the compositional semantic hierarchies present in real language models.

Limitations & Implications for AI Safety

This approach provides weak semantic structure—it is geometrically grounded but still falls short of true semantic understanding. We encode human-chosen semantic relationships into the statistical properties of the data, but the model still does not “understand” deception in any functional sense.

For practical AI safety research, this analysis suggests a two-stage validation approach: first, demonstrate SAE capabilities on increasingly complex synthetic statistical structures to establish baseline competence, then validate on real model activations with known safety-relevant semantic content. Only architectures that succeed at both stages should be considered reliable for safety-critical interpretability applications.

What we CAN test with statistical hierarchies:

  • Whether SAEs detect rare but statistically structured patterns
  • Scaling behavior when dangerous features are sparse
  • Whether transfer learning works for statistical signatures
  • Robustness of detection to feature correlations and hierarchical dependencies

What we CANNOT yet test:

  • Whether SAEs understand what “deception” means compositionally
  • Whether ablating “deception features” prevents deceptive reasoning
  • Whether constructed hierarchical relationships match real semantic hierarchies
  • Whether synthetic “scheming” features resemble real model representations
